SUMMARY

The Facebook Platform offers an ecosystem that already gave birth to many companies. It also presents a strong value for the social network, which monetizes the platform through sharing revenue models, but also because the platform generated several services that were then incorporated in the Facebook user experience.

However, the overall developer experience using the Facebook APIs is rather deplorable: design patterns are not standard, tutorials and documentation are not state of the art. Neither has the company shown stainless neutrality in the past, cutting off access to companies infringing their policies, and especially the non-core functionality replication clause, that remains a subjective matter.

Company Trust

It is water under the bridge since Facebook’s controversial IPO during the spring of 2012, when after ten days Facebook’s shares were traded half their 38$ initial price. Facebook repeatedly beat analysts forecasts, with mobile advertising revenues soaring between first and third quarters 2013. As a result, shares reached 50$ in late September. Facebook is more established than ever.

In October 2008, Facebook announced that it would set up its international headquarters in Dublin, Ireland. In September 2009, Facebook said that it had turned cash-flow positive for the first time.

Traffic to Facebook increased steadily after 2009. More people visited Facebook than Google for the week ending March 13, 2010.

In March 2012, Facebook announced App Center, a store selling applications that operate via the site. The store will be available to iPhone, Android and mobile web users.
Facebook held an initial public offering on May 17, 2012, negotiating a share price of $38 apiece. The company was valued at at $104 billion, the largest valuation to date for a newly listed public company.

Revenue Sources

Facebook generally has a lower clickthrough rate (CTR) for advertisements than most major Web sites. According to BusinessWeek.com, banner advertisements on Facebook have generally received one-fifth the number of clicks compared to those on the Web as a whole, although specific comparisons can reveal a much larger disparity. For example, while Google users click on the first advertisement for search results an average of 8% of the time (80,000 clicks for every one million searches), Facebook's users click on advertisements an average of 0.04% of the time (400 clicks for every one million pages).

Sarah Smith, who was Facebook's Online Sales Operations Manager, reports that successful advertising campaigns on the site can have clickthrough rates as low as 0.05% to 0.04%, and that CTR for ads tend to fall within two weeks. By comparison, the CTR for competing social network MySpace is about 0.1%, about 2.5 times better than Facebook's rate but still low compared to many other Web sites. The cause of Facebook's low CTR has been attributed to younger users enabling ad blocking software and being better at ignoring advertising messages, as well as the site being used more for the purpose of social communication as opposed to viewing content.

On pages for brands and products, however, some companies have reported CTR as high as 6.49% for Wall posts.

Business Model

The APIs business model depends on the apps and the APIs used:

  • Apps using Facebook Developer Payment (for real money or Facebook Credits virtual currency) share revenue through a tax. Facebook will earn a 30% service fee, plus any applicable sales tax or VAT, in connection with each Facebook Payments trans-action on the platform
  • Apps using Ads API: to integrate ads within an app, developers need to go through ad providers allowed by Facebook (https://developers.facebook.com/adproviders/). The fee perceived by Facebook included in these providers fees is undisclosed.

The APIs also drive product innovation at Facebook, as the past showed that many apps were bought out and then integrated in the service (list below).

ToS Abstract

The Facebook policies are divided in twelve paragraphs.

Paragraphs XI and XII state the license and definitions of the policies.

Paragraphs I to VII (Features and Functionality, Data Collection and Use, Content, Application Integration Points, Enforcement, Changes, Branding and Promotion Poli-cy) are basically a translation of the principles of the Platform, which Facebook de-scribes as:

  • Create a great user experience: Build social and engaging applications, Give users choice and control, Help users share expressive and relevant content
  • Be trustworthy: Respect privacy, Don't mislead, confuse, defraud, or surprise us-ers, Don't spam - encourage authentic communications
Most notable point is Reciprocity. As stated by Facebook officials after shutting off MessageMe, the point of reciprocity is that Facebook awaits personalized or social experiences built using Facebook APIs to bring value to the Facebook experience; hence, experiences over third party apps have to be easily shared back on Facebook.

Paragraphs VIII to X describe Advertising Guidelines, Facebook Developer Payments Terms and Ads API. If you monetize your third party app via advertising or through the Facebook Developer Payment program, we advise strongly to read these paragraphs.

Facebook has an engineering culture driven by “Stay focused, keep shipping and break things” and the partners of Facebook have to follow these changes.

Part of this retooling means that there’s a maintenance tax on whatever app has been created on the platform, since you have to pull your prized engineers off their projects to do constant maintenance and reintegration into the new viral channels. That’s just to keep up. It also means that what works today may not work tomorrow. Facebook APIs

  • Graph API

    The Graph API is a simple HTTP-based API that gives access to the Facebook social graph, uniformly representing objects in the graph and the connections between them. Most other APIs at Facebook are based on the Graph API.

  • FQL

    Facebook Query Language, or FQL, enables you to use a SQL-style interface to query the data exposed by the Graph API. It provides for some advanced features not available in the Graph API such as using the results of one query in another.

  • Open Graph

    The Open Graph API allows apps to tell stories on Facebook through a structured, strongly typed API.

  • Dialogs

    Facebook offers a number of dialogs for Facebook Login, posting to a person's timeline or sending requests.

  • Chat

    You can integrate Facebook Chat into your Web-based, desktop, or mobile instant messaging products. Your instant messaging client connects to Facebook Chat via the Jabber/XMPP service.

  • Localization and translation

    Facebook supports localization of apps.

  • Ads API

    The Ads API allows you to build your own app as a customized alternative to the Facebook Ads Manager and Power Editor tools.

  • Public Feed API

    The Public Feed API lets you read the stream of public comments as they are posted to Facebook.

  • Keyword Insights API

    The Keyword Insights API exposes an analysis layer on top of all Facebook posts that enables you to query aggregate, anonymous insights about people mentioning a certain term.

Authentication Design

Facebook uses OAuth2.0 as authentication method for its APIs. Globally the documentation is highly detailled with lots of examples and SDK to make it easier for developer and 3rd parties.

Especially about OAuth :

  • Facebook enables developers and 3rd-parties to have differents flows to implement their OAuth
  • OAuth 3 legged, OAuth 2 legged, device code, client credentials, enabling you to decide how do you want to manage the end-user account server side or client side, either the app account directly or connecting a 3rd device to Facebook (for example printing Facebook photos directly from an external printer)
  • Facebook OAuth has a revoke permissions enabling the end-user to be able to revoke the access to the application
  • Facebook OAuth has the cors call, enabling to connect on the client side.
Facebook and the RFC6749 standard

Not respecting the standards break the trust in the ecosystem, globally increase friction in overall API integration and make confusion in the head of developers and third parties about way to implement things where they will spend hours to understand and debug differences between each providers.

For the Facebook API part :

  • They call "access_token" instead of "oauth_token"
  • They use a comma "," as seprator in the scopes nstead of a whitespace
  • The use ‘ fb_exchange_token” instead of “refresh token”
Because of the popularity of Facebook Platform, some other API providers have considered Facebook OAuth as the defacto Standard of API and OAuth design and have followed this non standard pattern.<
