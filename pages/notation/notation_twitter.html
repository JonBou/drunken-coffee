SUMMARY

The Facebook Platform offers an ecosystem that already gave birth to many companies. It also presents a strong value for the social network, which monetizes the platform through sharing revenue models, but also because the platform generated several services that were then incorporated in the Facebook user experience.

However, the overall developer experience using the Facebook APIs is rather deplorable: design patterns are not standard, tutorials and documentation are not state of the art. Neither has the company shown stainless neutrality in the past, cutting off access to companies infringing their policies, and especially the non-core functionality replication clause, that remains a subjective matter.

Company Trust

It is water under the bridge since Facebook’s controversial IPO during the spring of 2012, when after ten days Facebook’s shares were traded half their 38$ initial price. Facebook repeatedly beat analysts forecasts, with mobile advertising revenues soaring between first and third quarters 2013. As a result, shares reached 50$ in late September. Facebook is more established than ever.

Ecosystem

The Facebook Platform was launched in 2007, and hence an early player in the public API world. It was conceived as a framework for software developers to create applications that interact with core Facebook features. Facebook’s API and SDK ecosystem is huge, and the structuration of the App center in May 2012 aims at helping best gaming and social apps to promote themselves. The ecosystem already gave rise to quoted companies such as Zynga.

But developers seems to quit the Facebook platform because of:

  • New virality constraints and rules
  • The APIs change too often
  • Competition is hard
  • Ad rates for traffic acquisition are higher than is the past
  • Less travtion from VCs on Facebook apps than for Mobile apps

Team Involvement

The Facebook Platform feeds are very active through Facebook and Twitter. The developer portal also efforts to reach the community of developers through its blog, events listing and roadmap.

Nonetheless, we have been informed by an undisclosed source that the Facebook API evangelist team quit in October 2013 and the position is not expected to be filled again.

Policy

Facebook has a strong record of externalizing innovation through its APIs. About twenty startups building services on top of Facebook were acquired, then shut down, while their innovations were incorporated in Facebook and their team members joined Facebook. It can sound unhappy, but this is a fair strategy to manage innovation through a public API.

However, Facebook also has a record of cutting off API access for infringing policies, particularly replicating core functionality, which Facebook used to justify revoking access to messaging apps, and Reciprocity, meaning that social services using Facebook API have to allow user to share experiences directly back on Facebook.

API Design and Documentation

Facebook has an engineering culture driven by “Stay focused, keep shipping and break things” and the partners of Facebook have to follow these changes.

Part of this retooling means that there’s a maintenance tax on whatever app has been created on the platform, since you have to pull your prized engineers off their projects to do constant maintenance and reintegration into the new viral channels. That’s just to keep up. It also means that what works today may not work tomorrow. If you are making important decisions on staffing, business models, financing, then a lot of uncertainty is introduced because your business might get disrupted by platform changes happening in a few months.

Facebook APIs global design do not respect standards for a major part. The features are very diverse, which makes versioning hard to follow. The Developers Portal is rich and provides abundant tools, widgets and SDKs as well as code samples, but no interactive documentation. As a result, and although Facebook is a very rich and wide environment to develop mashups, its APIs are very hard to use and maintain.
